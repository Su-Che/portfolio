The Research Problem Worth Solving

Pfizer's oncology research team processes thousands of research papers, clinical trial datasets, and biomarker datasets simultaneously. The existing workflow required researchers to manually cross-reference multiple databases, often spending days finding patterns that could accelerate drug candidate identification.

The goal: design an AI assistant interface that lets researchers ask complex questions in natural language and receive structured, visualised insights — dramatically compressing the time from question to discovery.

[ AI research interface — overview screen ]

Bridging the Gap Between Raw Data and Actionable Insight

The research team had access to vast amounts of data but lacked a unified interface to query it intelligently. Discovery sessions with the team surfaced four core friction points:

Data fragmentation
Researchers switching between 6+ tools to cross-reference data, losing context and time with every transition.
High cognitive load
Complex datasets presented in raw tabular form — no visual hierarchy or guided interpretation to reduce the mental burden.
Slow iteration cycles
Hypothesis testing took days. Researchers needed results in minutes to stay in flow state and maintain scientific momentum.
Trust in AI outputs
Scientists needed transparent reasoning behind AI suggestions — black-box responses were unacceptable in a clinical context.

Understanding How Scientists Actually Think

I conducted 24 in-depth interviews with oncology researchers, data scientists, and clinical trial managers across three continents. The research revealed distinct mental models around how scientists form hypotheses and expect data to respond.

"I don't want to learn a new query language. I want to ask the data a question the way I'd ask a colleague."

— Senior Research Scientist, Pfizer Oncology

Researchers spent 40% of their time on data retrieval, not analysis — an enormous opportunity for AI to intervene. Visualisations were static exports; scientists wanted live, exploratory charts they could interrogate in real time. And AI recommendations without provenance were immediately dismissed — citation trails were non-negotiable.

[ Research synthesis — affinity map and insight themes ]

Two Researchers, Two Very Different Needs

Dr. Priya Nair
Senior Oncology Researcher — 15 years experience, hypothesis-driven

Wants to test specific hypotheses quickly against large datasets. Values accuracy and source transparency above all. Frustrated by interfaces that require IT support to operate. Her primary question: "What does the data say about this drug-target combination?"

Marcus Heide
Computational Biology Lead — data-first, exploration-focused

Explores datasets without a fixed hypothesis, looking for unexpected patterns. Needs powerful filtering, custom visualisation types, and the ability to export findings. His primary question: "What patterns exist in this dataset that I haven't thought to look for?"

From Question to Insight in Seconds

The core interaction model: researchers type a question in plain English. The AI parses intent, queries the relevant datasets, and returns a visualised insight panel — with source citations, confidence indicators, and suggested follow-up queries.

[ Prompt interface — question entry with AI reasoning display ]

Three core surfaces were designed: the Prompt Interface (question entry + AI reasoning display), the Visualisation Canvas (interactive charts, network graphs, heatmaps), and the Insight Library (saved queries and collaborative annotations).

[ Visualisation canvas — interactive network graph ]
[ Insight library — saved queries and annotations ]

AI transparency was not a nice-to-have for expert users — it was the product. Without interpretable reasoning, the tool would have been rejected regardless of its accuracy. Every AI response shows its sources, confidence levels, and the datasets it queried.

Results That Changed How Pfizer Approaches Research

After a three-month pilot with 40 researchers, the results were significant. The research team responded positively to the introduction of the AI, expressing enthusiasm for its integration into their daily work and reporting measurable gains in productivity within the first month.

80% faster insights
Time to retrieve and synthesise research dropped from days to hours in controlled task testing.
64% time saving
Per research query — freeing scientists to focus on interpretation rather than retrieval.
92% user satisfaction
Post-pilot survey. The natural language interface was consistently cited as the standout feature.
3× research throughput
Teams could explore 3× as many drug-target hypotheses per week compared to the legacy workflow.

The biggest design lesson from this project: designing for domain experts requires deep humility. AI transparency is not a nice-to-have — it is the product. Without interpretable reasoning, the tool would have been rejected regardless of its accuracy.

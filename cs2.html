When Delivery Became a Race to the Bottom

Yodel, one of the UK's largest parcel carriers, faced an existential challenge: in a race-to-the-bottom price war, they needed a differentiated proposition. The brief was to define, design, and prototype a premium delivery service tier that would attract higher-value customers and brand partnerships.

This was as much a service design challenge as a product design one — the experience spans the retailer portal, the consumer-facing app, the driver interface, and physical delivery touchpoints.

What Customers Actually Want From Premium

I ran contextual research across three groups: end consumers, online retailers, and Yodel delivery drivers. Forty interviews, shadowed deliveries, and a quantitative survey of 800 consumers revealed the premium signals that truly mattered.

Predictability over speed
Consumers rated "precise 2-hour time windows" higher than next-day delivery as the #1 premium signal.
Communication quality
Real-time updates were expected — but the tone and clarity of comms was what set premium apart.
The doorstep moment
Consumers associated premium with the physical interaction — how a parcel is presented, packaging care, driver professionalism.
Retailer control
Retailers needed branded delivery experiences — white-label capability was a major commercial unlock.

Handheld terminals (HHT) frequently encountered software glitches, leading to suboptimal delivery routes due to inefficient route optimisation by the HHTs.

Tracking of the delivery from the service centre (SVC) by the SVC manager was difficult with inefficient software and lack of real time updates.

Customer Journey Map

Collaborative Creation with Stakeholders

Research alone doesn't change organisations — people do. To move from insight to action, I brought together a cross-functional group of stakeholders including product managers, operations leads, customer service representatives, depot managers, and senior leadership from Yodel. Rather than presenting findings top-down, I facilitated a structured co-design workshop where every voice shaped the solution.

Participants were organised into focused teams, each handed a specific "how might we" challenge derived directly from the pain points and opportunity areas we uncovered in research. This framing kept ideation grounded in real problems while opening space for creative thinking.

Safe and Fast Delivery
How might we ensure the customers get the parcels faster and secure?
Delivery Orchestration
How might we optimise the delivery for the delivery personnel to deliver efficiently?
Branded Delivery
How might we reinforce the brand values and upsell products during the delivery?

Through rapid ideation sessions, we generated a variety of innovative solutions for the prioritised challenges. The most promising ideas were prioritised and refined to create the cohesive journey. We narrowed down to one goal for the service.

"An integrated delivery ecosystem that connects customers, delivery personnel, and operations in real-time, creating a seamless and transparent experience."

Service Blueprinting

Creating a seamless and captivating experience is essential for keeping users engaged and satisfied. By refining the overall experience, we sought to elevate brand perception, lower customer churn rates, and clearly communicate the value of our service. The service blueprinting exercise was specifically undertaken to achieve these goals.

Setting Stages

Outlining the Stages of Service

We outlined key stages of the customer journey, detailing actions at each step. We added new stages inspired by innovative ideas. We mapped channels and touch-points for customer interaction, which can include the notifications, delivery vans or even the delivery staff.

Experience Detail

Crafting the Experience in Detail

We broke down the experience for each customer group and looked at the support tasks needed for creating the envisioned experience. These tasks could be things like transferring data, training staff, and setting up a team to handle the workload, among other stuff.

Service Blueprinting

Bringing Everything Together

By layering customer experiences across various channels and touch-points, as well as front-stage and back-stage activities, we developed a comprehensive service design blueprint that can be effectively implemented.

Service Blueprint

The service blueprint mapped every actor, touchpoint, and backstage process across the full premium delivery journey — from retailer booking through to post-delivery feedback. It became the single source of truth for cross-functional alignment across product, operations, and logistics teams.

Five Touchpoints, One Connected Experience

The premium service was designed as a connected experience across five distinct channels.

Retailer Portal
Booking, branding configuration and scheduling tools for retail partners to set up and manage the premium tier.
Consumer App
Live tracking, delivery preferences, and personalised communication — all in one branded experience.
Driver App
Premium job management, white-glove delivery protocols, and real-time route optimisation for drivers.
Physical Touchpoints
Standardised label design, premium packaging guidelines, and presentation standards at the doorstep.
Post-Delivery
Ratings, NPS surveys, and feedback loops that close the circle and drive continuous service improvement.

Customer-facing consumer app

Retailer & service centre manager — WISMO portal

The consumer app redesign introduced 2-hour delivery windows, live driver tracking with a named driver card, and a premium notification system with personalised language based on order value and retailer brand.

HHT Refresh

We suggested that the organisation switch to an Android handheld terminal and gave the interface a complete makeover to improve the experience for the delivery team.

Standardised Label Design

The label is used by all functions in the delivery process — from sort to billing to service centre operations. Delivery staff encountered daily frustrations with inconsistent green and yellow labels that caused scanning errors and slowed down operations. I created a new label design that is standardised across all label types, highlighting key information and ergonomically designed for quick, accurate scanning by delivery staff.

A simplified, unified parcel label that is visually clean, highlights the brand, and makes the entire process feel personal, professional and efficient. One label to replace all.

The UK's First Considered Premium Delivery Tier

The premium tier launched in partnership with major UK retailers in 2017 — the first time a UK parcel carrier had positioned a delivery service as a considered consumer choice rather than a logistics commodity. The end-to-end design spanned retailer onboarding, consumer-facing experience, driver tooling, and physical touchpoints.

What shipped

Premium consumer experience
Named driver card, 2-hour confirmed delivery windows, live tracking, and a personalised post-delivery rating flow — all delivered under retailer co-branding.
Retailer & operations portal
A white-label booking and scheduling portal enabling retail partners to configure and manage premium delivery tiers independently.
Driver tooling & label system
Refreshed HHT interface with improved route flow and a unified label standard replacing two inconsistent legacy formats across all depots.

Impact

Differentiated proposition in a commoditised market
Yodel became one of the first UK carriers to offer a commercially structured premium tier, providing a defensible alternative to competing on price alone.
Operational improvements at the last mile
Standardised labels and the HHT refresh reduced friction in depot workflows, with fewer scanning errors and clearer routing for delivery staff.
Foundation for retail partnerships
The retailer portal and co-branding framework enabled Yodel to onboard retail partners at scale — turning a design system into a commercial growth lever.

Learnings & Challenges

A project of this scale — spanning service design, product design, operations, and physical touchpoints — taught me as much as it delivered. Not everything went smoothly, and the constraints were often as instructive as the breakthroughs.

Challenges

Aligning a fragmented organisation
Yodel's operations, technology, and commercial teams had different definitions of success. Getting all three to agree on what "premium" meant — before a single pixel was designed — required more facilitation than I anticipated. Misalignment early would have derailed the project entirely.
Legacy systems as a design constraint
Several design decisions were shaped by backend limitations — the scheduling engine, depot scan infrastructure, and HHT firmware could not be replaced in the project timeline. Designing around legacy systems without letting them define the experience was a constant balancing act.
Retailer buy-in without a live product
We had to convince retail partners to commit to the premium tier before any consumer-facing product existed. Prototyping the retailer portal experience early — and making it feel credible — was critical to securing the first three launch partners.
Designing for the last mile at scale
Thousands of drivers, hundreds of depots, and millions of parcels per year meant that any design decision had operational consequences far beyond what a typical product change would carry. Edge cases that would be minor bugs in a consumer app became serious operational risks here.

Learnings

Service design is a political act
The hardest part of this project wasn't the design — it was navigating organisational dynamics. I learned that the blueprint is as much a political alignment tool as it is a design artefact. Getting people in a room to agree on who owns each touchpoint was often more valuable than the outputs of that session.
Drivers are a design audience too
Early research focused almost entirely on the customer. It wasn't until we spent time with drivers at depots that we understood how much the delivery experience depends on their workflow, tools, and morale. The HHT refresh and label standardisation emerged directly from that insight — and had outsized impact on outcomes.
Prototyping at the edges, not the centre
We learned to test exception states — what happens when the driver can't find the address, when a parcel is damaged, when the 2-hour window is missed — before testing the happy path. Edge case resilience turned out to be the primary driver of customer trust in the premium tier.
Physical touchpoints carry disproportionate weight
The label redesign and the named driver card were low-effort interventions relative to the app redesign, but customers noticed them most. The lesson: in a service experience, tangible physical moments create stronger emotional impressions than the most polished digital interface.
